### What dry-run CAN and CANNOT prove

Per `git help githooks` (CITED): the `pre-push` hook receives the push refs on
stdin and is invoked identically by `git push` and `git push --dry-run`. So:

- **Dry-run DOES exercise:** every line of `pre-push-strict.sh` (stdin lifecycle
  lines 40-43, sync-check at :145, the gate calls at :199-207, the EXIT trap
  installed at publish-gates.lib.sh:76-78).
- **Dry-run does NOT exercise:** the pack-data send phase that happens AFTER
  hook exit. If the SIGPIPE-141 originates from git writing pack data to an
  SSH pipe whose remote side has died, dry-run cannot observe it.

This caveat is the reason every "CONFIRMED" verdict in this spike is qualified
as `CONFIRMED-DRY-RUN` and the Wave 2 fix plan must close the gap with a
sentinel-commit real-push validation.

### Why minimal-blast `</dev/null` over structural `exec 0<&-` alone

The plan-author identifies two candidate fixes (RESEARCH §"Minimal SIGPIPE fix"):
the per-gate `</dev/null` and the single parent-side `exec 0<&-`. This spike
recommends **both**, in that order:

1. **`exec 0<&-` after PUSH_LINES `done`** — single structural barrier; closes
   the parent's stdin once the hook has consumed git's push-refs protocol.
   Single source of truth; defends against any future gate added without
   `</dev/null`.
2. **`</dev/null` at each of the 5 gate calls** — belt-and-suspenders. If a
   future maintainer accidentally re-opens stdin in the parent (e.g., a
   `read -p` prompt added before the gates), the gate-level redirects still
   isolate each child.
3. **`</dev/null` in the EXIT trap body** — independent fix for the trap-side
   leak (RESEARCH §Pitfall 3). Even if the gate-level fixes catch every leak,
   the trap-side patch is cheap, non-breaking, and addresses a separate
   potential SIGPIPE site.
